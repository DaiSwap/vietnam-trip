# Mums always say they don't mind

*Trying to plan a family trip to Vietnam, and what I learned about asking.*

---

Mums always say that, right? *"I don't mind, you all decide."* Mine said it the same way on the WhatsApp call about Vietnam — the same gentle shrug I've heard a hundred times. And then about ten minutes later, very quietly, she added: *"I heard Ha Giang is beautiful, no?"*

She did mind. Of course she did. She just wasn't going to be the one to say it first.

That's the moment I started thinking about this whole thing. We were six people trying to plan a trip — me, my parents, my brother, two cousins — and the family WhatsApp had forty-three messages by the end of the night, and somehow none of them were actually about *where we're going.* They were about who's coming, who's worried about flights, who saw a reel about Hoi An. Lots of opinions. Zero decisions.

I read the thread twice the next morning. I still didn't know what the plan was.

---

Here's the thing nobody tells you about family trips. They don't fail at the airport. They fail at the Google Doc.

You make the doc. You share it. You add columns — places, dates, budget. Maybe a tab for flights. Maybe a section for "things to do." And then you sit there refreshing it for three days while one person — usually you — actually fills anything in. The rest of the family has *read* it. They've absolutely read it. They've also said "looks good!" in the chat. They have not entered a single thing.

I used to think this was because people were lazy or busy or whatever. But honestly, that's not really it.

We show people too much stuff, ask them for too little, and we ask at the wrong moment. A 12-tab spreadsheet basically asks you to know what you want before you actually know what you want. It hands you a blank box and says *"tell us your opinion"* — but most people aren't sitting on opinions. They're sitting on vibes. They saw a photo once. They have a feeling about budget but not a number. They'd love to say yes to *something,* but the doc isn't asking them anything they know how to answer.

The hardest part isn't building the plan. It's figuring out what to show people, when, in a way that lets them say something true without first becoming experts.

---

When I started asking friends to help me think through it, a few of them wrote back the same way. *"I'll try, but I might not be the best person to choose."* Not as an excuse — more like a confession. Like they were being honest with me about something I hadn't quite thought through.

That line stayed with me for a few days.

Because the truth is: most family trips ARE decided by people who don't feel qualified. Nobody actually knows yet whether Ha Giang is going to be their favourite place ever, or whether they'd prefer Hoi An's old town. Nobody has a budget number ready when you ask. The "best person to choose" is a unicorn — and if you wait for that person, the trip never gets planned. Or worse, one person decides everything and everyone else just nods along and resents it quietly later.

So I started thinking — what if the whole thing was built FOR the friend who said *"I'll try anyway"*? Where the half-confident vote IS the real vote? Where tapping three places out of twelve is enough, because that's what most of us actually have to give? Where liking a photo counts as much as writing a whole paragraph — because it should?

Honestly, that's what I kept coming back to. Not the planning part. The *asking* part.

---

I should be honest about the part that still nags me, though.

When someone says *"I'm not the best person,"* they might also be telling me, gently, that they don't want to be asked at all. And me going *"no no, please, your input matters, just tap a few photos"* — that's still me pushing. It's just pushing in a friendlier voice. The polite exit they were trying to take? I'm kind of blocking it.

I don't know how to fix that. I'm not sure if making it easier to participate is helping them, or just making it harder to say no. Maybe both. I don't want to pretend I solved it.

---

So here's what I've been trying. Not as a finished thing — more as a *what if.*

What if planning didn't look like a form? What if it looked more like a story you were telling, one tap at a time. It asks your name first. That's it. Then how many people. Then your budget. Just plain questions — nothing weird, no forms with thirty fields.

Then you see a bunch of places — not a list, more like little postcards. You tap the ones that sound nice. You don't have to know why. You don't have to write a paragraph defending your choice. You just tap.

And anonymous. Nobody else sees what *you* picked. They only see the group's totals.

My brother opened it on his way back from work one evening. He tapped six places. Closed the tab. That was his whole input — and honestly, it was enough.

At the end, the site reads everything back to the group as a paragraph. Not a spreadsheet. Something like:

> *"You're leaning toward 14 days across 6 places. Starting in Ha Giang, working south to Ho Chi Minh City. Around ₹76,000 per person."*

The idea is that the synthesis is the *first sentence of the trip,* not a summary of decisions. And it gives people social cover too. The budget-hawk in the group can quote that paragraph without being the budget-hawk. The cousin who really didn't want to do Ha Giang can say *"the group is leaning that way"* without owning the preference. Most family trips, I think, get decided by what everyone was willing to say out loud — not by what they actually wanted. Those are two pretty different things.

I don't know yet if any of this actually works. It felt closer, though.

---

I sent it to my aunt last week. She hasn't opened it.

That might be the design failing. Or it might be the design being honest — she just doesn't want to be asked, and a friendlier interface doesn't change that.

To the friends who reached out and said *"I'll try, but I might not be the best person"* — that line is what made me build the whole thing the way I did. The taps are anonymous because of you. The questions are short because of you. Liking a photo counts as a real opinion because of you. If you've ever felt unqualified to plan something for a group of people you love — you were exactly who I was thinking about.

I'm writing this because I'd like to know if it actually helped. So if you've ever been on a family WhatsApp where everyone *"looked good!"* their way through a planning thread that decided nothing — or if you've ever been the person who said *"I'm not the best to choose,"* and meant it both ways — I'd love it if you tried it and told me what felt right and what didn't.

Especially the wrong parts. Those are the ones I can't see from inside.
